### Marketplace Platform Definition

* The Marketplace is a platform/page التابعة لـ “Bid”, proposed to be hosted on the subdomain `marketplace.bidapp.sa`.
* This page displays public tenders.
* Users can publish their own tenders and make them public through this page.

---

### User Journey

* The user starts by creating a Request for Proposal (RFP).
* During the publishing stage, the user selects “Marketplace” and submits the request.
* The tender only becomes public after communication and approval from the platform admin (Bid admin).

---

### Purchase Order (PO) Requirement

* The most critical step in the journey is adding a new document (Purchase Order) after submitting the request.
* This document can be added either by the user or by the admin.
* It is an official PO, signed and stamped by the client, confirming payment of a specific amount to the vendor based on the average tender value.
* This document is not visible to the public or other users—only the admin and the requester can view it.

---

### System Goals & Enforcement Policy

* The main goal of requiring the PO and admin approval is to prevent fake tenders or those that waste vendors’ time.
* Every tender published on “Bid” must be binding—the requester is required to select and award one of the participating vendors.
* If the requester does not select any vendor, a disclaimer message appears warning that their account will be permanently banned.
* The requester also cannot cancel the tender after it has been published.

### Benchmark Platforms

* **Etimad Platform:** The official government tenders platform in saudi arabia. Review the Public Tenders section, especially the “tender document purchase fee” concept (a fee paid to access tender files).

their marketplace platform looks like this structure:
1. Top Navigation Bar
The header contains three elements: the platform logo/brand (linking to the main portal), a login button that routes authenticated users to a private dashboard, and a breadcrumb showing "Tenders" as the current page. For your platform, this bar should persist across all pages and clearly separate public browsing (unauthenticated) from private actions (submitting bids, managing RFQs). A guest can browse freely; committing any action requires login.
2. Page Title + Search & Sort Controls
The "المنافسات" (Tenders) heading is paired immediately with a search bar and a results-per-page selector (6 / 12 / 18 / 24). For your platform, this zone is the primary filtering entry point — you'll want to extend it with filters for category, region, issuing entity, tender type, and deadline range. The per-page selector is a small but important UX detail that lets power users scan more listings at once.

4. Tender Listing Cards
Each card is a self-contained summary of one procurement opportunity and contains these fields:
Publication date — when the tender went live
Tender type badge — e.g., منافسة عامة (Open Tender) or شراء مباشر (Direct Purchase), displayed as a colored tag

Offer submission deadline — the hard close date/time
Bid opening date/time — when offers are unsealed and evaluated

For your platform, this card structure is your most important design unit. Each field maps directly to a procurement workflow stage. The countdown timer creates urgency. The document fee field indicates procurement formality level — free tenders are lower friction for suppliers.
5. Pagination Controls
Standard first / previous / numbered pages / next / last pattern. For your platform, consider infinite scroll with a "load more" fallback for mobile users, while keeping classic pagination for desktop power users who want to jump to a specific page. Clearly show total result count ("Showing 1–6 of 3,317 active tenders").
6. Footer

Support — phone number (19990), help center portal link, contact us link
Social channels — LinkedIn, Twitter/X, YouTube
Mobile app — App Store and Google Play download links, plus a sign language accessibility tool
For your platform, the support zone is critical for a procurement audience — buyers and suppliers need fast escalation paths. Adding a live chat widget here would outperform Etimad's phone-only support. The mobile app links signal that your platform should be mobile-ready from day one, especially for suppliers who check deadlines and notifications on the go.
Key Takeaways for Your Build
These are the highest-leverage decisions based on Etimad's structure:
Card schema is your data model — the fields on each tender card (deadline, type, entity, fee, reference number) should directly match your database schema for sourcing requests
Dual access model — public browsing + authenticated actions; never gate discovery behind a login wall
Tender type taxonomy — define your equivalents early (e.g., Open RFQ, Direct Purchase, Framework Agreement); this drives filtering and supplier matching logic
Countdown clocks — essential for urgency; calculate and render server-side so they're reliable
Statistics bar — populate this from day one even with small numbers; it signals legitimacy to early adopters

---

### Design & UI/UX Guidelines

* The design should feel familiar to users and not like a completely new, hard-to-use system.
* It should reflect Bid’s identity—sharp and modern—while avoiding the dull style seen in platforms like Etimad.
* The experience should be simple, fast, and user-friendly.
* At the same time, it should not be overly simplified to the point where large companies cannot input detailed and complex tender information.

---

### RFP Details

* The UI/UX for the RFP will remain identical to the existing RFP flow.
* All details and the brief stay the same—only the naming changes to “Tender.”
